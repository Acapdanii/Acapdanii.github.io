Motherboard Series (Important to Know)

Intel Motherboard Series:

  • H-Series – Basic/entry-level motherboards. Good for budget builds. No overclocking. Limited features.
  • B-Series – Mid-range motherboards. Better VRMs, more ports, good for gaming builds. Still no CPU overclocking.
  • Z-Series – High-end chipset. Allows CPU overclocking, better power delivery, more M.2 slots and features.
  • Q-Series – Business-oriented boards. Extra IT/management features. Not meant for gaming.

AMD Motherboard Series:

  • A-Series – Basic/entry-level (A320 / A520). No CPU overclocking. Good for simple or office builds.
  • B-Series – Most popular for gaming. Good performance, decent VRM, supports overclocking (B450 / B550 / B650).
  • X-Series – High-end chipset (X470 / X570 / X670). Best VRMs, more PCIe lanes, ideal for high-end Ryzen builds.

CPU (Processor)

Here comes the fun part, THE CPUs!!

How do you actually know if a CPU is good or not? Instead of just looking at the price, we can learn the basic naming first. Once you understand the name, you already know roughly how powerful the CPU is, which generation it is, and what it’s meant for.

Let’s start by looking at how the names are built:

Of course, I think most of you already know there are two main CPU brands: Intel and AMD Ryzen. Both of them use a similar naming idea (numbers to show how “high” the model is and what generation), but there are some small differences – you can see that from the pictures above.

I’ll just explain the important parts here.

Intel CPU Naming – i3, i5, i7, i9

For the older style like Core i3 / i5 / i7 / i9, the “i” number is the series level:

  • Core i3 – entry-level. Good for basic use: browsing, study, light office work.
  • Core i5 – mid-range. Very common for gaming + everyday use.
  • Core i7 – high-end. More cores/threads, better for heavy multitasking and high FPS gaming.
  • Core i9 – top consumer level. Lots of cores, great for streaming, editing, 3D work.

After the “i” number you’ll see digits like i5-12400 or i7-9700. The first digit (for example 9 in 9700 or 12 in 12400) tells you the generation. The rest of the numbers are the model – higher usually means slightly better within the same family.

New Intel Core Ultra Naming

Newer laptops and some desktops use names like Intel Core Ultra 7 165H. The idea is almost the same, just written differently:

  • Core Ultra – this is the new branding.
  • 7 – sub-series level (similar to the old i7: higher number = higher tier).
  • 1 in 165 – generation number.
  • 65 – model inside that generation (higher usually = better).
  • H – suffix. Tells you the type of chip (H usually means high-performance laptop CPU).

For example, a Core Ultra 5 is more like a mid-range chip, while a Core Ultra 7 is the higher tier. You can think of it just like the old i5 vs i7 idea.

AMD Ryzen Naming – Ryzen 3, 5, 7, 9

AMD Ryzen uses a very similar style: Ryzen 3 / 5 / 7 / 9.

  • Ryzen 3 – entry-level. Basic use, light gaming.
  • Ryzen 5 – mid-range. Super popular for gaming + daily use.
  • Ryzen 7 – high-end. Great for high FPS gaming and multitasking.
  • Ryzen 9 – top consumer level. Many cores, for heavy editing, streaming, and work.

The numbers after that, like Ryzen 5 5600 or Ryzen 7 7800X3D, work the same way:

  • The first digit (for example 5 in 5600 or 7 in 7800) is the generation.
  • The rest is the model – again, higher usually means better inside that series.

Threadripper (Bonus)

You might also see the name Ryzen Threadripper. This is AMD’s workstation / creator CPU line – way more cores and way more expensive. It’s not really meant for normal gaming builds, but good to know it exists.

So the next time you see a CPU like Core i5-12400 or Ryzen 7 5800X, you’ll already have an idea of where it stands: brand → series (3/5/7/9) → generation → model → suffix.

CPU SUFFIXES

CPU names sometimes end with letters. These are called suffixes, and they tell you what type of chip it is (power usage, overclocking ability, integrated graphics, etc).

Intel Desktop CPU Suffixes

  • K – Unlocked for overclocking. Higher performance.
  • F – No integrated graphics (needs a GPU).
  • T – Low-power desktop version.
  • S – Special edition / slightly boosted version.
  • X – High-End Desktop (HEDT) CPUs. For creators, workstation use. NOT meant for normal PC builders.
  • XE – Extreme Edition (very high-end, workstation-level CPUs).

⚠️ Some models combine suffixes, for example: Intel Core i5-12600KF. A combo like K + F becomes KF (unlocked + no iGPU).

Intel Laptop CPU Suffixes

  • U – Ultra-low power (thin & light laptops).
  • P – Mid-power thin laptops.
  • H – High-performance laptop CPU.
  • HX – Desktop-class performance in a laptop.
  • HK – High-performance + unlocked (rare).
  • G1 / G4 / G7 – iGPU strength indicator for older 10th/11th gen CPUs.

AMD Ryzen Desktop CPU Suffixes

  • No suffix – Normal desktop CPU.
  • X – Higher clock, higher performance.
  • XT – Slightly boosted/binned version of an X CPU.
  • G – Has integrated graphics (APU).
  • GE – APU with lower power use.
  • X3D – Has 3D V-Cache. Best gaming CPUs.

AMD Ryzen Laptop CPU Suffixes

  • U – Low-power, office & thin laptops.
  • HS – Balanced (performance + battery).
  • H – High-performance gaming laptops.
  • HX – Highest-power Ryzen laptop CPU.

Bonus: What is “G7” on Intel CPUs?

Older Intel laptop CPUs like i7-1165G7 use “G + number” to show GPU strength.

  • G1 – weakest iGPU
  • G4 – mid-level
  • G7 – strongest iGPU (Iris Xe)

So i7-1165G7 means: 11th gen CPU + the strongest integrated graphics for that generation.

WHICH SUFFIXES SHOULD BEGINNERS CARE ABOUT?

  • For Intel Desktop – Just remember: K (overclock), F (no graphics).
  • For Intel Laptop – Look for: H (gaming), U (battery life).
  • For Ryzen Desktop – Watch for: X (performance), G (has iGPU).
  • For Ryzen Laptop – H for gaming, U for battery.

Everything else is more “advanced builder knowledge”, so don’t worry too much for now.

RAM (Memory)

For RAM, it’s super simple. Every type of RAM has a unique gap (notch) on the stick that matches the slot on the motherboard. So you MUST check what type of RAM your motherboard supports.

TYPES OF RAM

  • DDR3 – Older generation RAM (usually 1333MHz – 1866MHz). Mostly found on older PCs, no longer recommended unless upgrading an old system.
  • DDR4 – Most common RAM type for builds from 2016–2023. Runs between around 2400MHz – 3600MHz for standard use. Good price and good speed.
  • DDR5 – Latest RAM standard (4800MHz – 8000MHz+). Faster and more efficient. Mostly used in modern Intel 12th–14th gen and Ryzen 7000 builds.

SO WHAT IS THE MINIMUM AMOUNT OF RAM?

Well, it depends on what you do. If all you're doing is browsing the internet, watching videos, doing assignments, light coding, or office work like PowerPoint or Word, 8GB of RAM is more than enough.

On the other hand, if you want to play games smoothly, the minimum is 16GB. Heavier games can use more than 16GB, especially modded games like Minecraft with large modpacks. AAA titles on max settings can easily use above 20GB of RAM.

DOES RAM SPEED MATTER THAT MUCH?

Yes… but not as much as you think. it matters, but not dramatically. For example, if you compare DDR5 4800MHz and DDR5 6000MHz, you might only see a small difference of around 5–10 FPS in games.

BONUS

You might see things like DDR4 3200MHz CL40. So what is CL40?

CL stands for CAS Latency — basically the number of cycles it takes between your processor asking for data from the RAM and the RAM actually delivering it.

Just think of it like how fast the RAM hands over data to the CPU.

LOWER CL VALUE = FASTER

You will see variations like CL40, CL32, CL30, CL26… And YES — CAS latency affects performance more than raw MHz speed.

For example: DDR5 4800MHz CL26 can actually perform better than DDR5 6000MHz CL40.

Storage (SSD / HDD)

Storage (SSD / HDD)

When it comes to storage, there are a few different types and each of them has their own pros and cons.

STORAGE TYPES

  • HDD (Hard Disk Drive) – Old-school spinning drive. Very cheap per GB, but very slow. Good for storing files, not great for Windows or games.
  • SATA SSD – Uses the SATA cable. Much faster than HDD, but slower than NVMe. Still perfectly fine for normal users and light gaming.
  • M.2 SATA SSD – small M.2 shape, but speed is limited to normal SATA. Faster than HDD, similar to 2.5" SATA SSD, but smaller form factor.
  • M.2 NVMe SSD – Uses the M.2 slot and NVMe protocol. Very fast read/write speeds. Best choice for Windows and modern games.

    • ⚠️ NOTE: M.2 SATA and M.2 NVMe DO NOT use the same slot. Make sure to check your motherboard for the correct M.2 support before buying!

DOES STORAGE SPEED MATTER?

My answer is YES because it affects how fast your whole PC feels. If you install Windows 10 on an HDD, it might take around 1–3 minutes just to boot into Windows. On the other hand, if you install it on an NVMe M.2 SSD, it can boot in around 10 seconds (depending on your SSD and system).

WHAT SHOULD YOU GO FOR?

If you mostly browse the internet, do assignments, watch videos, and do light coding, a SATA SSD is perfectly fine. Just make sure your Windows is installed on that SSD.

If you are doing heavy task such as 3D rendering, play a lot of games, especially heavy titles like Cyberpunk and other AAA games, an M.2 NVMe SSD is probably your best choice for smooth loading and less stutter.

IS HDD THAT BAD NOW?

Yes and no. HDDs are still okay for storing light data such as pictures, documents, and videos. But for gaming, you might want to avoid using HDD.

Installing games on an HDD can become your worst nightmare because of stutters. Why does it happen? Because the HDD can’t load the game textures fast enough due to its slow speed, the game may freeze or hitch while it waits for the data.

SO SHOULD I BUY AN HDD?

If all you're doing is storing pictures, videos, and files, then yes, an HDD is still useful as a cheap storage drive.

But if you're planning to install your games or Windows on an HDD? Hell no. Use an SSD (SATA or NVMe) for that.

BONUS

NVMe M.2 SSDs have different speeds based on their generation:

  • Gen 3 NVMe – Up to around 3500 MB/s read/write.
  • Gen 4 NVMe – Up to around 7000 MB/s (or more) read/write.

Both are already very fast for gaming, but Gen 4 is on another level for big file transfers and heavy workloads.

IMPORTANT

Each SSD type has its own specific slot. Before buying, always check your motherboard to see:

  • How many SATA ports you have for 2.5" SATA SSDs / HDDs.
  • How many M.2 slots you have, and whether they support NVMe, SATA, or both.

This saves you from buying a drive that your motherboard can’t actually use.

CPU Cooler

CPU COOLERS – KEEPING YOUR CPU COOL

The CPU cooler’s job is simple: take heat away from your CPU so it doesn’t overheat or throttle. A hot CPU = lower performance and shorter lifespan.

STOCK COOLER

A stock cooler is the cooler that comes in the box with some CPUs. It’s not the best, but it can do the job for basic use.

  • Okay for light usage and light gaming.
  • Can get quite hot under heavy load.
  • Often a bit noisy when the CPU is working hard.

Some CPUs do not come with a stock cooler. This is usually because those CPUs run hotter, and the manufacturer expects you to buy a better cooler.

⚠️ Always check: “Cooler included” or “Boxed with cooler” before buying a CPU.

TYPES OF CPU COOLERS

  • AIO (All-In-One Liquid Cooler) – BEST AND SAFEST
  • Custom Liquid Cooling – GOOD BUT HARD TO MAINTAIN
  • Tower Air Cooler
  • Stock Cooler – LAST OPTION

AIO (All-In-One) Liquid Cooler – BEST & SAFEST

An AIO is a closed liquid cooling system with a pump, radiator, and fans. It’s sealed from the factory, so you don’t fill any water yourself.

  • Great cooling performance.
  • Cleaner look (radiator mounted at front/top).
  • Good for high-end CPUs and overclocking.
  • No need to maintain liquid (just make sure fans stay clean).

Custom Liquid Cooling – GOOD BUT HARD TO MAINTAIN

This is the “full custom loop” with tubes, reservoir, pump, and separate blocks. It looks amazing and can cool very well, but:

  • More expensive.
  • Harder to install.
  • You need to maintain it (check for leaks, maybe change fluid).

This is not recommended for beginners unless you really want to learn and are ready to spend extra time and money.

Tower Air Cooler

A tower air cooler is a big heatsink with heatpipes and a fan attached. It’s one of the best options for most people.

  • Cheaper than AIO in many cases.
  • Very reliable (no liquid, no pump).
  • Can be very quiet if you choose a good model.
  • Some big ones are heavy and tall – make sure your case has enough space.

Stock Cooler – LAST OPTION

Stock coolers are fine if:

  • You’re on a tight budget.
  • You’re not doing heavy gaming or CPU-intensive tasks.

But if you want lower temps, less noise, and better performance, upgrading to at least a tower air cooler is a very good idea.

✅ Simple rule: For gaming builds, at least get a good air cooler or a basic AIO instead of relying on stock.

Power Supply (PSU)

POWER SUPPLY (PSU)

The PSU is one of the most important parts of your PC, even though many beginners ignore it. A bad PSU can kill your whole PC, so choose wisely.

WHAT DOES WATTAGE MEAN?

Wattage tells you how much power your PSU can output. A 650W PSU can deliver up to 650 watts to your system.

  • 450–550W – For office PCs or low-end builds
  • 600–750W – Ideal for mid-range gaming builds
  • 750–850W – For high-end GPUs like RTX 4070/4080
  • 1000W+ – Extreme builds (4090, heavy overclocking)

WHAT IS 80+ RATING?

80+ rating tells you how efficient your PSU is. Higher rating = less heat, more stable power.

  • 80+ Bronze – Minimum recommended
  • 80+ Gold – Best value for gaming PCs
  • 80+ Platinum / Titanium – Super efficient (overkill for most)

MODULAR vs NON-MODULAR

  • Non-modular: All cables attached. Harder to manage.
  • Semi-modular: Some removable cables.
  • Fully modular: All cables removable. Best airflow & cable management.

⚠️ NOTE: Avoid cheap, unbranded PSUs.A bad PSU can actually destroy your whole entire PC, not just itself. Always pick a trusted brand (Corsair, Cooler Master, Seasonic, FSP, Thermaltake).

HOW DO I KNOW HOW MANY WATTS I NEED?

It’s actually very simple. Your total wattage depends on your CPU + GPU, because those are the two parts that use the most power.

Here’s a very easy beginner rule:

  • Office PC (no GPU) → 300–450W is enough
  • Budget GPU (GTX 1650 / RX 6400) → 450–550W
  • Mid-range GPU (RTX 3060 / 4060 / RX 6600) → 550–650W
  • Upper mid-range (RTX 3070 / 4060 Ti / 4070) → 650–750W
  • High-end (RTX 4080 / RX 7900 XT) → 750–850W
  • Extreme GPUs (RTX 4090) → 1000W or higher

⚠️ NOTE: Always leave at least 100–150W of headroom. Example: If your system uses around 400W, get **550W or 600W**, not 450W.

If you want to be extra accurate, you can search for: “PSU calculator” from OuterVision or Cooler Master. But for beginners, the simple chart above is perfectly enough.

PC Case

PC CASE BASICS

The PC case is not just for looks. It decides how easy your build is, how cool your parts run, and whether your GPU and cooler can even fit inside.

CASE SIZES

  • Full Tower – Very big. Supports E-ATX, ATX, mATX, ITX. Best for custom loops & huge GPUs.
  • Mid Tower – Most common size. Supports ATX, mATX, and sometimes E-ATX.
  • Micro-ATX Case – Smaller, made for mATX boards (some also fit ATX).
  • Mini-ITX Case – Very small, only fits ITX boards. Building can be harder.

⚠️ NOTE: Your motherboard size must match your case support (ATX, mATX, ITX, etc), and your GPU length + CPU cooler height must fit the case too.

AIRFLOW & VENTILATION

Airflow is how air moves through your case to cool down your parts. A case with bad airflow = higher temps and more fan noise.

  • Mesh front panel – Better airflow, cooler temps.
  • Solid / glass front panel – Looks nice, but usually worse airflow.
  • Top ventilation – Great for exhausting hot air from CPU and GPU.

INTAKE vs EXHAUST FANS

Case fans have two jobs: intake (pull cool air in) and exhaust (push hot air out).

  • Intake fans – usually at the front or bottom of the case.
  • Exhaust fans – usually at the back and top of the case.

Simple and effective setup:

  • 2–3 intake fans at the front
  • 1 exhaust fan at the back
  • Optional: extra exhaust at the top for hot air rising

⚠️ NOTE: Try to have slightly more intake than exhaust for positive pressure. This helps reduce dust getting sucked in through random gaps.

OTHER THINGS TO CHECK

  • Dust filters – Make cleaning easier (front, top, and bottom filters are nice).
  • GPU clearance – Some big GPUs won’t fit in small cases.

Graphics Card (GPU)

HOW TO READ GPU NAMES

Just like CPUs, graphics cards also follow a naming scheme. Once you understand the name, you can roughly tell how powerful the GPU is and which generation it belongs to.

NVIDIA GPU NAMING (GTX / RTX)

Let’s take this example: RTX 4070

  • RTX – Brand/series (RTX means it supports ray tracing and DLSS)
  • 40 – Generation (40-series = newer than 30-series
  • 70 – Model tier (higher number = stronger GPU)

General rule for NVIDIA:

  • RTX 50 / 60 – Entry to mid-range (1080p gaming)
  • RTX 70 – High-end (1440p gaming)
  • RTX 80 / 90 – Enthusiast level (4K gaming, heavy workloads)

AMD GPU NAMING (RX)

Example: RX 7800 XT

  • RX – AMD Radeon series
  • 7 – Generation (7000 series)
  • 800 – Model tier (higher = stronger)
  • XT – Higher-performance version

General rule for AMD:

  • RX x600 – Entry to mid-range gaming
  • RX x700 / x800 – High-end gaming
  • RX x900 – Enthusiast cards

OC vs NON-OC GPU

  • OC (Overclocked) – Factory overclocked. Slightly higher performance and slightly higher power usage.
  • NON-OC – Runs at stock speed. Performance difference is usually very small.

For beginners: OC or NON-OC doesn’t matter much. Buy whichever is cheaper or has better cooling.

LHR / LH GPUs

LHR (Lite Hash Rate) GPUs were made to reduce crypto-mining performance.

  • LHR / LH does NOT affect gaming performance
  • Only affects mining (which most gamers don’t care about)

If you’re gaming → you can ignore LHR completely.

DO ALL GPUS NEED EXTRA POWER?

No. Some GPUs can run using power directly from the motherboard, while others require extra power from the PSU.

  • No extra power needed – Low-end GPUs (GT 1030, GTX 1650 non-Super)
  • Needs extra power – Most GPUs (RTX, RX cards)

GPUs that need extra power will use:

  • 6-pin
  • 8-pin
  • 6 + 8-pin
  • 12VHPWR / 12+4-pin (newer high-end NVIDIA GPUs)

⚠️ NOTE: Always check if your PSU has the required GPU power cables before buying a graphics card.

Common Questions

WiFi / Internet Capabilities?

WAYS TO GET INTERNET ON A PC

There are 4 main ways to get internet on your PC:

  • 1. LAN cable (Ethernet)
  • 2. Built-in WiFi on the motherboard
  • 3. PCIe WiFi card
  • 4. USB WiFi adapter

1. LAN CABLE (BEST OPTION)

LAN (Ethernet) is the fastest and most stable way to get internet. Perfect for gaming, streaming and downloading.

You’ll see cable types like Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a, Cat7:

  • Cat5e – Up to 1 Gbps. Already enough for most home users.
  • Cat6 – Better shielding, can do up to 10 Gbps on shorter runs.
  • Cat6a / Cat7 – Even better shielding, meant for higher speeds / longer cables.

✅ If you play online games or care about ping, always use LAN if possible.

2. BUILT-IN WIFI ON THE MOTHERBOARD

Some motherboards come with built-in WiFi and antennas included. This is super convenient: you just install the board, screw in the antenna, and connect to WiFi.

  • Clean look (no extra card)
  • Usually supports WiFi 5 or WiFi 6 / 6E on newer boards

3. PCIE WIFI CARD

A PCIe WiFi card is an add-on card you plug into your motherboard’s PCIe slot. It works just like built-in WiFi but is bought separately.

  • Better and more stable than USB WiFi
  • Often has external antennas at the back of the PC
  • Good choice if your motherboard has no built-in WiFi

4. USB WIFI ADAPTER (LAST OPTION)

USB WiFi adapters plug into your USB port and give you WiFi instantly. They are easy to use but usually have the worst stability and speed.

  • Good for temporary use or very light browsing
  • Not recommended for heavy gaming or big downloads

⚠️ If you can choose: LAN > Built-in WiFi > PCIe WiFi > USB WiFi (from best to worst for gaming and stability).
